# User Research Summary 

# ThreadVault
> Users rely heavily on memory to manage their wardrobes, leading to frequent outfit-planning friction and redundant purchases. A lightweight, visual digital closet shows strong early demand.

Version: v1 (MVP)
Research Method: User survey (Google Form)
Participants: 17 respondents
Date: January 2026

## 1. Research Goal
The goal of this research was to understand how users currently manage their wardrobes, find the issues they face in outfit planning and clothing organization, and assess whether a digital closet would be useful for reducing everyday friction. The findings were used to inform MVP scope and prioritize foundational wardrobe management features.

## 2. Research Methodology
Research was conducted via an online survey distributed to students and young professionals. The survey included a mix of multiple-choice and open-ended questions focused on current wardrobe habits, pain points, and interest in potential features. Responses reflect early-stage validation and are intended to guide MVP decisions rather than represent a statistically broad sample.

## 3. Participant Profile
Participants primarily consisted of students and young professionals who manage their wardrobes independently. Most respondents rely on mental tracking or physical organization methods, with limited use of digital tools beyond photos or notes. This group was well-suited for early validation, as they frequently make independent outfit decisions and experience recurring wardrobe-related friction.

## 4. Key Insights
### Insight 1: Most users rely on memory or physical organization
The majority of respondents reported using mental tracking or physical organization to keep track of their clothing. Many acknowledged forgetting about items they own, indicating a lack of a reliable system for visual reference.
#### Why this matters:
This validates the need for a centralized, visual wardrobe system as a core product value.

### Insight 2: Outfit decision-making is a frequent pain point
Many respondents reported struggling to decide what to wear either almost every day or several times a week. The most commonly cited difficulties included remembering what items they own and knowing what pieces go together.
#### Why this matters:
This confirms that outfit planning friction is a recurring problem, not an occasional inconvenience.

### Insight 3: Visual reference is the most helpful decision aid
When asked what helps most with choosing an outfit, respondents frequently selected seeing all options visually or understanding how items pair together. This preference appeared consistently across different wardrobe organization styles.
#### Why this matters:
A visual-first approach is critical for reducing cognitive load during outfit planning.

### Insight 4: Redundant purchases are common
A significant number of respondents indicated that they have purchased clothing only to later realize they already owned something similar. This suggests low awareness of existing wardrobe inventory.
#### Why this matters:
Improving wardrobe visibility can help users make more intentional purchasing decisions.

### Insight 5: Interest in a digital closet exists, but effort is a concern
Most respondents indicated that a digital closet would be useful. However, common barriers included concerns about the time required to upload items and privacy around photos.
#### Why this matters:
The MVP should remain lightweight and avoid unnecessary complexity that could discourage adoption.

## 5. Impact on Product Decisions
- **Centralized wardrobe cataloging** was prioritized in v1 to address reliance on memory and physical organization (Insights 1 & 3).
- **Custom categories and browsing by category** were included to support organization preferences and reduce outfit-planning friction (Insights 2 & 3).
- **Item counts and visual overviews** were added to improve awareness of wardrobe composition and reduce redundant purchases (Insight 4).
- **Automation and AI-driven outfit generation** are planned for future iterations, following validation of core wardrobe management workflows in v1 (Insight 5).

These decisions directly informed the scoped feature set for ThreadVault v1.

## 6. Open Questions & Future Research
- How much friction does item uploading introduce during onboarding?
- Would users engage more with outfit creation features once their wardrobe is fully cataloged?
- What level of automation would users find helpful without feeling overwhelmed?
- How do privacy concerns impact long-term usage?

Future research may include usability testing, onboarding experiments, or follow-up surveys after extended use.

## 7. Summary
Early user research indicates that wardrobe management is largely handled through mental tracking or physical organization, leading to frequent outfit-planning friction and underutilized clothing. Respondents showed strong interest in a visual, centralized wardrobe system, validating the core direction of ThreadVault v1. These insights informed a deliberately scoped MVP focused on foundational wardrobe visibility and organization.
